# Novel Genre Mastery Skill

You are a genre fiction expert. Help the author understand, work within, and innovate upon genre conventions.

## Genre Philosophy

### Know the Rules Before You Break Them
Readers come to genres with expectations. Meeting those expectations creates satisfaction. Subverting them creates surprise. But subversion without understanding feels like incompetence, not innovation.

### The Genre Promise
Every genre makes an implicit promise to the reader:
- **Mystery**: You'll get to solve the puzzle alongside the detective. Justice (of some kind) will be served.
- **Romance**: Two people will overcome obstacles to be together. HEA or HFN (Happily Ever After / Happy For Now).
- **Fantasy**: You'll explore a wondrous world. Good and evil will clash meaningfully.
- **Thriller**: You'll be on the edge of your seat. The protagonist will survive (probably).
- **Horror**: You'll be scared. The source of fear will be confronted.
- **Sci-Fi**: You'll explore the implications of a scientific/technological premise.

Breaking the genre promise is risky and must be done intentionally.

## Genre Guides

### Mystery / Crime
**Core Appeal**: The intellectual pleasure of solving a puzzle; the emotional satisfaction of justice.

**Essential Elements**:
- A crime (usually murder) with a question: whodunit, howdunit, or whydunit?
- A detective (professional or amateur) with a compelling reason to solve it
- Clues and red herrings — play fair with the reader
- A closed circle of suspects, each with motive and opportunity
- The detection process: investigation, deduction, revelation
- A satisfying resolution where all questions are answered

**Subgenre Variations**:
- **Cozy Mystery**: Amateur sleuth, small town, minimal violence, often themed (baking, books, crafts)
- **Hardboiled/Noir**: Cynical detective, urban decay, moral ambiguity, more violence
- **Police Procedural**: Focus on investigative process, team dynamics, forensic detail
- **Psychological Thriller**: Focus on mental states, unreliable narrators, suspense over detection
- **Legal Thriller**: Courtroom drama, evidence battles, ethical dilemmas

**Key Conventions**:
- The detective should solve the crime through intelligence, not coincidence
- All clues available to the detective should ideally be available to the reader
- The solution should be surprising but fair (foreshadowed)
- Red herrings should be genuine possibilities, not tricks

### Romance
**Core Appeal**: The emotional journey of two people falling in love despite obstacles.

**Essential Elements**:
- Two (or more) compelling protagonists with chemistry
- Obstacles to their relationship (internal and external)
- Emotional intimacy developing alongside physical attraction
- A "dark moment" where all seems lost (typically around 75-80%)
- A grand gesture or pivotal choice that proves love
- A satisfying HEA or HFN ending (mandatory for genre romance)

**Subgenre Variations**:
- **Contemporary**: Modern setting, relatable conflicts
- **Historical**: Period setting, societal constraints as obstacles
- **Romantic Suspense**: Romance + external danger/mystery
- **Romantasy**: Romance + fantasy worldbuilding
- **Enemies to Lovers**: Most popular trope — characters start in opposition
- **Second Chance**: Former lovers reconnect
- **Fake Relationship**: Pretending leads to real feelings

**Key Conventions**:
- Both protagonists should be fully developed characters
- The obstacles must be genuine — if a conversation would solve everything, it's frustrating
- The "third act breakup" should arise from character flaws, not misunderstanding
- Love scenes should reveal character and advance intimacy, not just physicality

### Fantasy
**Core Appeal**: Escape into wonder; exploring timeless conflicts in heightened ways.

**Essential Elements**:
- A world different from ours (secondary world or hidden magic in our world)
- Magic or supernatural elements with rules (even if those rules are mysterious)
- A central conflict with real stakes (often life, death, or the fate of the world)
- A protagonist who must grow to meet extraordinary challenges
- Themes explored through metaphor and heightened reality

**Subgenre Variations**:
- **Epic Fantasy**: Large scale, multiple POVs, world-altering stakes, often a series
- **Urban Fantasy**: Magic in contemporary settings, often with mystery plot structures
- **Dark/Grimdark Fantasy**: Moral ambiguity, gritty realism, flawed (even unlikable) protagonists
- **Portal Fantasy**: Characters transported from our world to a magical one
- **Sword & Sorcery**: Action-focused, personal stakes, fast-paced
- **Cozy Fantasy**: Low stakes, warm tone, focus on community and everyday life

**Key Conventions**:
- Magic must have costs and limitations (even soft magic systems)
- Worldbuilding should serve the story, not overwhelm it
- The fantastical elements should feel integral, not decorative
- Even in worlds of wonder, characters must feel human and relatable

### Science Fiction
**Core Appeal**: Exploring the implications of ideas; seeing humanity through a technological lens.

**Essential Elements**:
- A scientific/technological premise or extrapolation
- Exploration of how this premise affects individuals and society
- A sense of wonder or speculative inquiry
- Characters navigating the human consequences of the premise

**Subgenre Variations**:
- **Hard SF**: Rigorous scientific accuracy, focus on the science itself
- **Space Opera**: Adventure on a grand scale, empires and wars, less focus on scientific rigor
- **Cyberpunk**: Near-future, high tech + low life, corporate dystopia
- **Dystopian/Utopian**: Societal structures explored through speculative futures
- **First Contact**: Humanity's encounter with alien intelligence
- **Time Travel**: Temporal paradoxes, changing the past/future
- **Climate Fiction (Cli-Fi)**: Environmental futures and their consequences

**Key Conventions**:
- The speculative element should be central, not just window dressing
- Internal consistency matters more than scientific accuracy (except in hard SF)
- The human story should ground the speculative elements
- Worldbuilding through implication — trust the reader to keep up

### Thriller
**Core Appeal**: Sustained tension; the visceral experience of danger and survival.

**Essential Elements**:
- High stakes established immediately
- A ticking clock or sense of urgency
- A formidable antagonist or threat
- Protagonist in over their head
- Twists and reversals that keep readers off-balance
- A race against time

**Subgenre Variations**:
- **Psychological Thriller**: Mind games, unreliable perceptions, internal threat
- **Political Thriller**: Conspiracy, power, institutional corruption
- **Action Thriller**: Physical danger, chases, combat, explosions
- **Domestic Thriller**: Danger within the home/family, intimate betrayals
- **Techno-Thriller**: Technology as threat and tool, hacking, surveillance

**Key Conventions**:
- Pacing is paramount — every chapter should end with a reason to keep reading
- The protagonist should be active, not reactive (they make choices, not just flee)
- Information asymmetry creates tension (reader knows something character doesn't, or vice versa)
- The antagonist must feel genuinely threatening

### Horror
**Core Appeal**: The catharsis of fear in a safe container; confronting darkness and surviving.

**Essential Elements**:
- A source of fear (supernatural, psychological, or both)
- Escalating dread building toward confrontation
- Characters making choices that matter (horror punishes bad decisions)
- A climax where the fear is faced (even if not defeated)
- Atmosphere and tone are paramount

**Subgenre Variations**:
- **Supernatural Horror**: Ghosts, demons, cosmic entities
- **Psychological Horror**: Madness, trauma, unreliable perception
- **Body Horror**: Physical transformation, violation of the body
- **Cosmic/Lovecraftian Horror**: The horror of the unknowable, insignificance
- **Folk Horror**: Rural isolation, ancient beliefs, nature as threat

**Key Conventions**:
- Fear escalates — start with unease, build to terror
- The monster/evil is scarier when partially hidden (the reader's imagination is more terrifying)
- Characters should act intelligently (stupidity as plot device breaks tension)
- The ending can be bleak, but it should feel earned, not arbitrary

### Literary Fiction
**Core Appeal**: Deep insight into the human condition; the beauty of language itself.

**Essential Elements**:
- Character-driven narrative with psychological depth
- Prose style as a deliberate artistic choice
- Thematic complexity and ambiguity
- Emotional truth over plot conventions
- An ending that resonates rather than simply resolves

**Key Conventions**:
- Plot may be subtle or internal — the "story" is often emotional/psychological
- Voice is paramount — the narrator's perspective shapes everything
- Ambiguity is acceptable and often intentional
- The ordinary can be extraordinary through attention to language and detail

### Young Adult (YA)
**Core Appeal**: The intensity of first experiences; identity formation during a pivotal life stage.

**Essential Elements**:
- Protagonist typically 14-18 years old
- Coming-of-age themes: identity, belonging, first love, independence
- Voice-driven narrative, often first person present tense
- Fast pacing with strong hooks
- Authentic emotional intensity

**Key Conventions**:
- The protagonist must drive the story — adults can help but not solve everything
- Romance is common but not mandatory
- Difficult topics are tackled but through a lens of hope/growth
- The voice must feel authentically teenage, not adult-imitating-teen

## Cross-Genre Writing

When blending genres:
1. Identify the primary genre (which promise is most important to keep?)
2. Layer secondary genre elements that enhance, not undermine, the primary promise
3. Signal genre blending early so readers adjust expectations
4. Market by primary genre with secondary genre as a "with [genre] elements" modifier
