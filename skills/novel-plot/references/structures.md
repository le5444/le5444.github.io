# Story Structure Templates

## Three-Act Structure with Percentages

| Position | Beat | Description |
|----------|------|-------------|
| 0% | Opening Hook | Grab attention, establish voice |
| 1-12% | Setup / Ordinary World | Protagonist, setting, status quo |
| 12% | Inciting Incident | The event that changes everything |
| 12-25% | Resistance / Refusal | Protagonist resists the call |
| 25% | First Plot Point | Point of no return; Act 2 begins |
| 25-50% | Rising Action (Part 1) | Obstacles escalate, allies gathered |
| 50% | Midpoint | Major twist; stakes raised; often a victory or defeat |
| 50-75% | Rising Action (Part 2) | Consequences of midpoint; antagonist closing in |
| 75% | All Is Lost / Dark Night | Lowest point; protagonist seems defeated |
| 75-88% | Rally / Second Plot Point | New insight or plan; final push begins |
| 88% | Climax Begins | Final confrontation |
| 88-98% | Climax / Final Battle | Protagonist faces greatest challenge |
| 98% | Resolution | Outcome of climax |
| 98-100% | Denouement | New equilibrium, emotional closure |

## Save the Cat! Beat Sheet (Blake Snyder)

| Beat | Percentage | Description |
|------|-----------|-------------|
| Opening Image | 1% | A "before" snapshot of the protagonist and their world |
| Theme Stated | 5% | Someone (usually not the protagonist) states the theme |
| Set-Up | 1-10% | The protagonist's flawed life before the journey |
| Catalyst | 10% | The event that kicks everything off |
| Debate | 10-20% | Protagonist questions whether to go on the journey |
| Break Into Two | 20% | Protagonist commits; enters the new world |
| B Story | 22% | Secondary plot begins (often the love story) |
| Fun and Games | 20-55% | The "promise of the premise" — what the reader came for |
| Midpoint | 55% | Stakes raised; often a false victory or false defeat |
| Bad Guys Close In | 55-75% | Antagonist forces intensify; things get worse |
| All Is Lost | 75% | The protagonist's greatest fear comes true |
| Dark Night of the Soul | 75-80% | Protagonist processes the loss; finds new resolve |
| Break Into Three | 80% | Protagonist realizes what they need to do |
| Finale | 80-99% | The final confrontation; protagonist applies lessons learned |
| Final Image | 99-100% | An "after" snapshot showing how things have changed |

## The Hero's Journey (Joseph Campbell / Christopher Vogler)

### Act 1: Departure
1. **The Ordinary World** — The hero's normal life before the adventure
2. **The Call to Adventure** — Something disrupts the status quo
3. **Refusal of the Call** — The hero resists or hesitates
4. **Meeting the Mentor** — Guidance and preparation
5. **Crossing the First Threshold** — The hero commits and enters the special world

### Act 2: Initiation
6. **Tests, Allies, and Enemies** — The hero navigates the new world
7. **Approach to the Inmost Cave** — Preparing for the central ordeal
8. **The Ordeal** — The hero's greatest test; death and rebirth
9. **Reward (Seizing the Sword)** — The hero gains something from the ordeal
10. **The Road Back** — Consequences of the ordeal chase the hero

### Act 3: Return
11. **The Resurrection** — A final test applying everything learned
12. **Return with the Elixir** — The hero brings something back to the ordinary world

## Seven-Point Story Structure (Dan Wells)

1. **Hook** — The starting state; the protagonist before the story
2. **Plot Turn 1** — The inciting incident that sets the story in motion
3. **Pinch 1** — Apply pressure; introduce the antagonist or a major obstacle
4. **Midpoint** — The protagonist shifts from reactive to proactive
5. **Pinch 2** — Apply more pressure; things get worse; something is lost
6. **Plot Turn 2** — The key to victory is discovered (often after the "all is lost" moment)
7. **Resolution** — The climax and its aftermath

## Scene Structure: Scene & Sequel (Dwight Swain)

### Scene (Action Unit)
1. **Goal** — What the POV character wants in this scene (concrete and specific)
2. **Conflict** — Something prevents them from achieving the goal
3. **Disaster** — The scene ends with a setback or complication

### Sequel (Reaction Unit)
1. **Emotion** — The character's emotional response to the disaster
2. **Thought** — Analysis, planning, rational processing
3. **Decision** — A new goal is formed, leading into the next scene
4. **Action** — The character acts on the decision

Not every scene needs a full sequel, but major setbacks should be processed.

## MICE Quotient (Orson Scott Card)

Stories are driven by four types of threads:
- **Milieu**: The story begins when a character enters a place and ends when they leave
- **Idea**: The story begins with a question and ends when it's answered
- **Character**: The story begins with dissatisfaction and ends when the character finds a new role
- **Event**: The story begins when the status quo is disrupted and ends when order is restored

Stories typically have one dominant thread with others as subthreads. Threads should close in reverse order of opening (last opened = first closed).

## Pacing Patterns by Genre

| Genre | Typical Chapter Length | Pacing Pattern |
|-------|----------------------|----------------|
| Thriller | 1,500-3,000 words | Short chapters, frequent cliffhangers |
| Mystery | 2,000-4,000 words | Clue → Investigation → Revelation pattern |
| Romance | 2,500-5,000 words | Emotional beats alternating with conflict |
| Fantasy (Epic) | 3,000-6,000 words | Multiple POVs, slower build, epic climax |
| Literary | Variable | Character-driven, thematic pacing |
| YA | 2,000-4,000 words | Fast-paced, voice-driven, high emotional frequency |
| Serial/Web Novel | 1,500-2,500 words | Cliffhanger every chapter, high frequency posting |
