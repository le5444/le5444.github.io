# Literary Techniques & Rhetorical Devices Reference

## Point of View Techniques

### First Person
Narrator is a character ("I") within the story. Intimate but limited.
- **Unreliable Narrator**: The narrator's account can't be fully trusted (mental illness, bias, deliberate deception, limited knowledge)
- **Epistolary**: Story told through documents — letters, diary entries, emails, transcripts
- **Stream of Consciousness**: Unfiltered flow of thoughts, associations, and sensory impressions

### Second Person
Narrator addresses the reader as "you." Rare; creates immediacy but can feel gimmicky.
- Best for: experimental fiction, interactive narrative, instructional tone
- Example: "You walk into the room and immediately sense something wrong."

### Third Person Limited
Narrator is outside the story but accesses one character's perspective per scene.
- Most common in modern fiction
- Variable narrative distance: can be very close (free indirect discourse) or moderately distant

### Third Person Omniscient
Narrator knows everything and can enter any character's mind. Classic 19th-century style.
- Narrator may have their own personality, opinions, commentary
- Best for: large casts, sweeping historical narratives, irony

### Free Indirect Discourse
Blended third-person technique where the narrator's voice merges with the character's inner voice.
- No "she thought" or "he wondered" — just the thought itself in the narrative flow
- Example: "The party was unbearable. Why had she come? The wine was cheap, the conversation worse."

## Narrative Techniques

### In Medias Res
Starting in the middle of the action, then filling in backstory through flashbacks or exposition.

### Framing Device
A story within a story (e.g., a character telling a tale, a discovered manuscript).

### Non-Linear Narrative
Events presented out of chronological order for thematic or suspense purposes.

### Multiple POV / Polyphony
Multiple narrators telling different parts of the story, each with distinct voice and perspective.

### Unreliable Narration Types
- **The Innocent**: Child or naive character who doesn't understand what they're reporting
- **The Madman**: Mental illness or altered state distorts perception
- **The Liar**: Deliberately deceiving the reader for personal gain
- **The Amnesiac**: Gaps in memory create mystery and unreliability

## Rhetorical & Literary Devices

### Sound Devices
- **Alliteration**: Repeated initial consonant sounds ("dark and dreary")
- **Assonance**: Repeated vowel sounds ("the rain in Spain")
- **Consonance**: Repeated consonant sounds, not at word beginnings
- **Onomatopoeia**: Words that imitate sounds ("buzz", "hiss", "crack")

### Structural Devices
- **Anaphora**: Repetition of words at the start of successive clauses ("We shall fight... we shall fight...")
- **Epistrophe**: Repetition at the end of successive clauses
- **Chiasmus**: Reversal of structure for effect ("Ask not what your country can do for you...")
- **Polysyndeton**: Multiple conjunctions for rhythmic effect ("and... and... and...")
- **Asyndeton**: Omission of conjunctions for speed and urgency
- **Parallelism**: Similar grammatical structure across phrases or clauses

### Meaning Devices
- **Metaphor**: Direct comparison without "like" or "as"
- **Simile**: Comparison using "like" or "as"
- **Personification**: Giving human qualities to non-human things
- **Metonymy**: Substituting something associated with the thing meant ("the crown" for monarchy)
- **Synecdoche**: Part representing the whole ("all hands on deck")
- **Irony**: Saying the opposite of what is meant; situation contradicting expectation
- **Oxymoron**: Contradictory terms together ("deafening silence", "sweet sorrow")
- **Paradox**: Apparently contradictory statement that reveals a truth
- **Hyperbole**: Deliberate exaggeration
- **Understatement / Litotes**: Deliberate minimization for effect
- **Euphemism**: Mild expression substituted for a harsh one

### Emotional Devices
- **Pathos**: Appeal to emotion
- **Bathos**: Sudden shift from elevated to commonplace (intentional or unintentional)
- **Pathetic Fallacy**: Nature reflecting human emotion ("the grieving sky wept")
- **Zoomorphism**: Attributing animal qualities to humans or objects

## Dialogue Techniques

### Subtext
What characters mean beneath what they say. The gap between words and intent.

### Exposition Through Dialogue
Characters reveal information naturally, but avoid "As you know, Bob" exchanges where characters state what they both already know.

### Dialogue Attribution Styles
- **"Said" is invisible**: The reader's eye skips over it — it's the default tag
- **Action beats**: Replace tags with action ("I'm leaving." She grabbed her coat.)
- **No attribution**: In rapid exchanges between two speakers, attribution can drop
- **Adverbial tags**: Use sparingly. "Said softly" is fine; "expostulated vehemently" is distracting.
