# Character Archetypes & Templates

## Classic Archetypes (Based on Jung / Vogler)

| Archetype | Function | Examples |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **The Hero** | Protagonist who sacrifices for others | Harry Potter, Katniss, Frodo |
| **The Mentor** | Guides and trains the hero | Dumbledore, Gandalf, Morpheus |
| **The Threshold Guardian** | Tests the hero before major transitions | Bouncers, gatekeepers, sentinels |
| **The Herald** | Brings the call to adventure | Letters, messengers, prophecies |
| **The Shapeshifter** | Loyalties are uncertain; keeps hero guessing | Snape, Catwoman, Gollum |
| **The Shadow** | Represents the dark side; the antagonist | Voldemort, Sauron, Darth Vader |
| **The Ally / Sidekick** | Supports the hero; often provides contrast | Ron & Hermione, Samwise, Dr. Watson |
| **The Trickster** | Creates mischief; challenges the status quo | Fred & George Weasley, Loki, Jack Sparrow |

## Personality Frameworks

### The Enneagram for Writers
Use the Enneagram to give characters consistent fears, motivations, and stress responses.

| Type | Core Fear | Core Desire | Stress Direction | Growth Direction |
|------|-----------|-------------|------------------|------------------|
| 1: Reformer | Being corrupt/evil | To be good, right, balanced | → 4 (moody, self-absorbed) | → 7 (spontaneous, joyful) |
| 2: Helper | Being unloved/unwanted | To be loved and needed | → 8 (controlling, aggressive) | → 4 (authentic, creative) |
| 3: Achiever | Being worthless/failure | To be valuable and admired | → 9 (disengaged, apathetic) | → 6 (loyal, committed) |
| 4: Individualist | Having no identity | To be unique and authentic | → 2 (people-pleasing) | → 1 (principled, purposeful) |
| 5: Investigator | Being helpless/incapable | To be competent and knowledgeable | → 7 (scattered, impulsive) | → 8 (confident, decisive) |
| 6: Loyalist | Being without support | To be safe and secure | → 3 (workaholic, image-focused) | → 9 (calm, trusting) |
| 7: Enthusiast | Being trapped in pain | To be happy and satisfied | → 1 (perfectionistic, critical) | → 5 (focused, insightful) |
| 8: Challenger | Being controlled/harmed | To protect self and be in control | → 5 (withdrawn, secretive) | → 2 (caring, open-hearted) |
| 9: Peacemaker | Loss and separation | To have peace and harmony | → 6 (anxious, suspicious) | → 3 (energized, effective) |

## Character Arc Templates

### The Positive Change Arc (The Hero's Journey Arc)
```
Starting State: Character believes FALSE BELIEF
↓
Inciting Incident: Event challenges the false belief
↓
Rising Conflict: False belief causes problems; reality keeps contradicting it
↓
Dark Night of the Soul: False belief fails completely; character sees the truth
↓
Transformation: Character embraces the TRUTH
↓
Climax: Character acts on the truth, demonstrating change
```

### The Tragic Arc
```
Starting State: Character has a FLAW they refuse to acknowledge
↓
Rising Power/Success: Flaw seems to be a strength; character is rewarded despite it
↓
Warning Signs: Flaw starts causing collateral damage; character ignores warnings
↓
Point of No Return: Character doubles down on flaw instead of changing
↓
Climax: Flaw leads to catastrophe; character is destroyed or loses everything
```

### The Flat Arc (The Testing Arc)
```
Starting State: Character already knows the TRUTH
↓
Testing: The world challenges their truth; they face pressure to abandon it
↓
Midpoint: Truth seems to fail; character wavers
↓
Affirmation: Character recommits to truth despite cost
↓
Climax: Character's truth changes the world around them
```

## Character Voice Development

### Voice Dimensions
- **Vocabulary level**: Simple, moderate, sophisticated, specialized jargon
- **Sentence complexity**: Short and punchy → Long and philosophical
- **Formality**: Casual, conversational, formal, archaic
- **Humor**: Dry, sarcastic, goofy, dark, none
- **Pace**: Fast-talking, measured, hesitant, rambling
- **Cultural markers**: Regional dialect, class indicators, professional shorthand
- **Emotional expression**: Open, guarded, explosive, repressed

### Voice Distinction Exercise
For each major character, write the same scene from their perspective. If their voices aren't immediately distinguishable, they need more differentiation.
